Interated Integration

Introduction

Suppose we have a integrable volume \(S\) in three dimensions. To find it's volume, we must choose an order of integration. If we have integration variables \(a,b,c\), then a possible integration ordering is \(a\) then \(b\) then \(c\) which is represented by \(\iiint \text{d}a\text{d}b\text{d}c\). Below two possible integration orderings are implemented for several volumes. Notice that, however, not all orderings are equally nice. The goal of this exercise is to give you an intuition for recognizing good orders of integrations. By a good ordering, I mean that it would be the easiest to write and calculate by hand.

Moving a slider will translate a plane through \(S\) and show the intersection, where all the plane's points have that variable's value on the slider. For example, consider the ordering \(\iiint \text{d}a\text{d}b\text{d}c\). Moving an \(a\)-slider translates the plane \(a = a_i\) and shows the blue intersection between it and \(S\), where \(a_i\) is the value along the \(y\)-slider. Since \(a\) is the first to be integrated, this intersection will be two-dimensional. Subsequently moving the \(b\)-slider translates the plane \(b = b_i\) and shows the green intersection between it, \(a = a_i\), and \(S\), where \(b_i\) is the value along the \(b\)-slider. Since \(b\) is the second to be integrated, this intersection will be one-dimensional.

Steinmetz Solid

Here, we have the solution to

$$ \begin{cases} 1 &\geq x^2 + y^2 \\ 1 &\geq y^2 + z^2 \end{cases}, $$

which is, in fact, the intersection of those two cyliders. As with the previous examples, find the best order of integration for this volume. Make use of the pattern's you've noticed so far, and relate them to the symmetries of the volume itself.
